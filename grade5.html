üìê Fractions and Mixed Numbers

Understanding Fractions

A fraction represents part of a whole. It has two parts: the numerator (top number) shows how many parts you have, and the denominator (bottom number) shows how many equal parts the whole is divided into.

Example: Pizza Fractions

If you cut a pizza into 8 equal slices and eat 3 slices, you ate 3/8 of the pizza.

‚Ä¢ Numerator = 3 (slices eaten)

‚Ä¢ Denominator = 8 (total slices)

Types of Fractions

Proper Fractions

The numerator is smaller than the denominator (less than 1 whole).

Examples: 1/2, 3/4, 5/8, 7/10

Improper Fractions

The numerator is equal to or greater than the denominator (equal to or greater than 1 whole).

Examples: 5/4, 8/3, 9/9, 15/7

Mixed Numbers

A whole number combined with a proper fraction.

Examples: 2 1/3, 4 2/5, 1 3/4

Converting Between Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers

Converting Improper Fractions to Mixed Numbers

  1. Divide the numerator by the denominator
  2. The quotient becomes the whole number
  3. The remainder becomes the new numerator
  4. Keep the same denominator

Example: Convert 17/5 to a Mixed Number

17 √∑ 5 = 3 remainder 2

So 17/5 = 3 2/5

Converting Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions

  1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator
  2. Add the numerator to this product
  3. Write the sum over the original denominator

Example: Convert 4 3/7 to an Improper Fraction

(4 √ó 7) + 3 = 28 + 3 = 31

So 4 3/7 = 31/7

Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Same Denominators

When fractions have the same denominator, add or subtract the numerators and keep the same denominator.

Examples:

2/7 + 3/7 = 5/7

5/9 - 2/9 = 3/9 = 1/3

Different Denominators

When fractions have different denominators, you need to find a common denominator first.

  1. Find the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the denominators
  2. Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions with the common denominator
  3. Add or subtract the numerators
  4. Simplify if possible

Example: 1/4 + 1/6

LCM of 4 and 6 = 12

1/4 = 3/12 and 1/6 = 2/12

3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12

Multiplying Fractions

To multiply fractions, multiply the numerators together and multiply the denominators together.

Example: 2/3 √ó 4/5

(2 √ó 4)/(3 √ó 5) = 8/15

Dividing Fractions

To divide fractions, multiply by the reciprocal (flip the second fraction).

Example: 3/4 √∑ 2/5

3/4 √ó 5/2 = 15/8 = 1 7/8

Converting Between Fractions and Decimals

Fraction to Decimal

Divide the numerator by the denominator.

Examples:

Write the decimal as a fraction with the appropriate denominator based on the place value.

Examples:

To compare decimals, line up the decimal points and compare digit by digit from left to right.

Compare: 0.47 and 0.5

0.47 compared to 0.50

In the tenths place: 4 < 5

Therefore: 0.47 < 0.5

Adding and Subtracting Decimals

  1. Line up the decimal points
  2. Add zeros if needed to make the same number of decimal places
  3. Add or subtract as usual
  4. Bring down the decimal point

Example: 12.75 + 8.3

Multiplying Decimals

  1. Multiply as if they were whole numbers
  2. Count the total number of decimal places in both numbers
  3. Place the decimal point that many places from the right in your answer

Dividing Decimals

When dividing by a whole number, place the decimal point directly above in the quotient. When dividing by a decimal, make the divisor a whole number first.

Example: 8.4 √∑ 2.1

üìà Introduction to Percentages

What is a Percentage?

A percentage is a way to express a fraction with a denominator of 100. The word "percent" means "per hundred" or "out of 100."

Understanding Percent

25% means 25 out of 100, or 25/100, or 0.25

If 25% of students in your class wear glasses, that means 25 out of every 100 students wear glasses.

Converting Between Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

Decimal to Percentage

Move the decimal point two places to the right and add the % symbol.

Examples:

0.75 = 75%

0.4 = 40%

0.08 = 8%

Percentage to Decimal

Move the decimal point two places to the left and remove the % symbol.

Examples:

60% = 0.60 = 0.6

15% = 0.15

3% = 0.03

Fraction to Percentage

Convert the fraction to a decimal first, then to a percentage.

Examples:

1/4 = 0.25 = 25%

3/5 = 0.6 = 60%

7/10 = 0.7 = 70%

Common Percentage Equivalents

It's helpful to memorize these common conversions:

Common Conversions

  • 1/2 = 0.5 = 50%
  • 1/4 = 0.25 = 25%
  • 3/4 = 0.75 = 75%
  • 1/5 = 0.2 = 20%
  • 1/10 = 0.1 = 10%
  • 1/3 ‚âà 0.33 ‚âà 33%
  • 2/3 ‚âà 0.67 ‚âà 67%

Finding Percentages of Numbers

To find a percentage of a number, convert the percentage to a decimal and multiply.

Example: Find 30% of 80

30% = 0.30

0.30 √ó 80 = 24

So 30% of 80 is 24

Using Percentages in Real Life

Test Scores

If you got 18 out of 20 questions correct on a test:

18/20 = 0.9 = 90%

Sales and Discounts

If a shirt costs $40 and is 25% off:

Discount = 25% of $40 = 0.25 √ó $40 = $10

üìê Geometry: Angles, Polygons, and Symmetry

Understanding Angles

An angle is formed when two lines or rays meet at a point called the vertex. Angles are measured in degrees (¬∞).

Types of Angles

  • Acute Angle: Less than 90¬∞ (sharp angle)
  • Right Angle: Exactly 90¬∞ (corner of a square)
  • Obtuse Angle: Greater than 90¬∞ but less than 180¬∞ (wide angle)
  • Straight Angle: Exactly 180¬∞ (straight line)
  • Reflex Angle: Greater than 180¬∞ but less than 360¬∞

Angle Examples in Real Life

  • Right angle: Corner of a book, intersection of floor and wall
  • Acute angle: Slice of pizza, letter 'V'
  • Obtuse angle: Opening of a book, letter 'L' turned sideways

Measuring Angles

We use a protractor to measure angles. Place the center point on the vertex and align one ray with the zero line.

Polygons

A polygon is a closed shape made up of straight lines. The lines are called sides, and where they meet are called vertices.

Types of Polygons

  • Triangle: 3 sides
  • Quadrilateral: 4 sides
  • Pentagon: 5 sides
  • Hexagon: 6 sides
  • Octagon: 8 sides
  • Decagon: 10 sides

Special Triangles

By Side Length

  • Equilateral Triangle: All three sides equal
  • Isosceles Triangle: Two sides equal
  • Scalene Triangle: All sides different lengths

By Angles

  • Right Triangle: Has one 90¬∞ angle
  • Acute Triangle: All angles less than 90¬∞
  • Obtuse Triangle: One angle greater than 90¬∞
Triangle Fact: The angles in any triangle always add up to 180¬∞! If you know two angles, you can always find the third by subtracting from 180¬∞.

Special Quadrilaterals

Rectangle

  • 4 right angles
  • Opposite sides equal and parallel

Square

  • 4 right angles
  • All sides equal
  • Special type of rectangle

Parallelogram

  • Opposite sides parallel and equal
  • Opposite angles equal

Rhombus

  • All sides equal
  • Opposite sides parallel
  • Special type of parallelogram

Trapezoid

  • Exactly one pair of parallel sides

Symmetry

A shape has symmetry if it looks exactly the same after being flipped, turned, or moved in a certain way.

Line Symmetry (Reflection Symmetry)

A shape has line symmetry if it can be folded along a line so that both halves match exactly.

Examples of Line Symmetry

  • Butterfly - 1 line of symmetry (vertical)
  • Square - 4 lines of symmetry
  • Circle - infinite lines of symmetry
  • Rectangle - 2 lines of symmetry

Rotational Symmetry

A shape has rotational symmetry if it looks the same after being turned (rotated) by less than a full turn around its center.

Examples of Rotational Symmetry

  • Square - looks the same every 90¬∞ turn
  • Equilateral triangle - looks the same every 120¬∞ turn
  • Regular hexagon - looks the same every 60¬∞ turn

Congruent and Similar Shapes

Congruent Shapes

Shapes that are exactly the same size and shape. They can be flipped, turned, or moved, but they must match perfectly when placed on top of each other.

Similar Shapes

Shapes that have the same shape but different sizes. All corresponding angles are equal, and all corresponding sides are proportional.

Example

All squares are similar to each other (same shape), but only squares with the same side length are congruent (same size).

üß© Problem Solving with Multiple Operations

Understanding Multi-Step Problems

Many real-life math problems require more than one operation to solve. These are called multi-step problems because you need to do several calculations in the right order.

Example: School Fundraiser

Your class sells 45 boxes of cookies for $8 each and 30 boxes of chocolates for $12 each. How much money did your class raise in total?

Step 1: Cookie money = 45 √ó $8 = $360

Step 2: Chocolate money = 30 √ó $12 = $360

Step 3: Total money = $360 + $360 = $720

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

When solving problems with multiple operations, we must follow the correct order:

PEMDAS Rules

  1. Parentheses first
  2. Exponents (powers)
  3. Multiplication and Division (left to right)
  4. Addition and Subtraction (left to right)

Example: 6 + 3 √ó 4 - 2

Step 1: Multiply first ‚Üí 3 √ó 4 = 12

Step 2: Now we have 6 + 12 - 2

Step 3: Add and subtract left to right ‚Üí 6 + 12 = 18

Step 4: 18 - 2 = 16

Problem-Solving Strategies

The Four-Step Process

  1. Understand: What is the problem asking? What do I know?
  2. Plan: What operations do I need? In what order?
  3. Solve: Do the calculations step by step
  4. Check: Does my answer make sense?

Multi-step percentage problems are common in real life.

üî¢ Number Patterns and Sequences

What are Number Patterns?

A number pattern is a sequence of numbers that follows a specific rule. Understanding patterns helps us predict what comes next and solve problems more efficiently.

Simple Pattern Example

2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, ...

Pattern Rule: Add 2 to each number

Next numbers: 14, 16, 18

Types of Number Patterns

In arithmetic sequences, the same number is added (or subtracted) each time.

  • 5, 10, 15, 20, 25... (add 5)
  • 100, 90, 80, 70, 60... (subtract 10)

In geometric sequences, each number is multiplied (or divided) by the same amount.

Examples of Geometric Sequences

  • 2, 6, 18, 54, 162... (multiply by 3)
  • 80, 40, 20, 10, 5... (divide by 2)
  • 1, 4, 16, 64, 256... (multiply by 4)

Finding Pattern Rules

Steps to Find a Pattern Rule

  1. Look at the differences between consecutive numbers
  2. Check if the differences are the same (arithmetic)
  3. If not, check if there's a multiplication rule (geometric)
  4. Look for other relationships (squares, adding previous terms, etc.)

Rule: Start with 5, add 7 each time

üî¨ Advanced Life Processes and Classification

Characteristics of Living Things

All living things share certain characteristics that make them different from non-living things. Scientists use these characteristics to identify and classify life on Earth.

The Seven Life Processes

  1. Movement: All living things can move in some way
  2. Respiration: Using oxygen to release energy from food
  3. Sensitivity: Responding to changes in the environment
  4. Growth: Increasing in size and complexity
  5. Reproduction: Creating new individuals of the same species
  6. Excretion: Removing waste products from the body
  7. Nutrition: Taking in and using food for energy and growth

üåø Plant and Animal Adaptations

What are Adaptations?

Adaptations are special features that help plants and animals survive in their environments. These features develop over many generations and help organisms find food, avoid danger, and reproduce successfully.

Types of Adaptations

  • Structural: Body parts and features (sharp teeth, thick fur)
  • Behavioral: Actions and habits (migration, hibernation)
  • Physiological: Internal body processes (producing venom, regulating temperature)

üìê Fractions and Mixed Numbers

Understanding Fractions

A fraction represents part of a whole. It has two parts: the numerator (top number) shows how many parts you have, and the denominator (bottom number) shows how many equal parts the whole is divided into.

Example: Pizza Fractions

If you cut a pizza into 8 equal slices and eat 3 slices, you ate 3/8 of the pizza.

‚Ä¢ Numerator = 3 (slices eaten)

‚Ä¢ Denominator = 8 (total slices)

Types of Fractions

Proper Fractions

The numerator is smaller than the denominator (less than 1 whole).

Examples: 1/2, 3/4, 5/8, 7/10

Improper Fractions

The numerator is equal to or greater than the denominator (equal to or greater than 1 whole).

Examples: 5/4, 8/3, 9/9, 15/7

Mixed Numbers

A whole number combined with a proper fraction.

Examples: 2 1/3, 4 2/5, 1 3/4

Converting Between Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers

Converting Improper Fractions to Mixed Numbers

  1. Divide the numerator by the denominator
  2. The quotient becomes the whole number
  3. The remainder becomes the new numerator
  4. Keep the same denominator

Example: Convert 17/5 to a Mixed Number

17 √∑ 5 = 3 remainder 2

So 17/5 = 3 2/5

Converting Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions

  1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator
  2. Add the numerator to this product
  3. Write the sum over the original denominator

Example: Convert 4 3/7 to an Improper Fraction

(4 √ó 7) + 3 = 28 + 3 = 31

So 4 3/7 = 31/7

Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Same Denominators

When fractions have the same denominator, add or subtract the numerators and keep the same denominator.

Examples:

2/7 + 3/7 = 5/7

5/9 - 2/9 = 3/9 = 1/3

Different Denominators

When fractions have different denominators, you need to find a common denominator first.

  1. Find the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the denominators
  2. Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions with the common denominator
  3. Add or subtract the numerators
  4. Simplify if possible

Example: 1/4 + 1/6

LCM of 4 and 6 = 12

1/4 = 3/12 and 1/6 = 2/12

3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12

Multiplying Fractions

To multiply fractions, multiply the numerators together and multiply the denominators together.

Example: 2/3 √ó 4/5

(2 √ó 4)/(3 √ó 5) = 8/15

Dividing Fractions

To divide fractions, multiply by the reciprocal (flip the second fraction).

Example: 3/4 √∑ 2/5

3/4 √ó 5/2 = 15/8 = 1 7/8

üìà Introduction to Percentages

What is a Percentage?

A percentage is a way to express a fraction with a denominator of 100. The word "percent" means "per hundred" or "out of 100."

Understanding Percent

25% means 25 out of 100, or 25/100, or 0.25

If 25% of students in your class wear glasses, that means 25 out of every 100 students wear glasses.

Converting Between Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

Move the decimal point two places to the right and add the % symbol.

Move the decimal point two places to the left and remove the % symbol.

Examples:

Fraction to Percentage

Convert the fraction to a decimal first, then to a percentage.

Examples:

1/4 = 0.25 = 25%

Understanding Angles

An angle is formed when two lines or rays meet at a point called the vertex. Angles are measured in degrees (¬∞).

Types of Angles

  • Acute Angle: Less than 90¬∞ (sharp angle)
  • Right Angle: Exactly 90¬∞ (corner of a square)
  • Obtuse Angle: Greater than 90¬∞ but less than 180¬∞ (wide angle)
  • Straight Angle: Exactly 180¬∞ (straight line)
  • Reflex Angle: Greater than 180¬∞ but less than 360¬∞

Angle Examples in Real Life

  • Right angle: Corner of a book, intersection of floor and wall
  • Acute angle: Slice of pizza, letter 'V'
  • Obtuse angle: Opening of a book, letter 'L' turned sideways

We use a protractor to measure angles. Place the center point on the vertex and align one ray with the zero line.

Polygons

A polygon is a closed shape made up of straight lines. The lines are called sides, and where they meet are called vertices.

Symmetry

A shape has symmetry if it looks exactly the same after being flipped, turned, or moved in a certain way.

Line Symmetry (Reflection Symmetry)

A shape has line symmetry if it can be folded along a line so that both halves match exactly.

Examples of Line Symmetry

  • Butterfly - 1 line of symmetry (vertical)
  • Square - 4 lines of symmetry
  • Circle - infinite lines of symmetry
  • Rectangle - 2 lines of symmetry

Rotational Symmetry

A shape has rotational symmetry if it looks the same after being turned (rotated) by less than a full turn around its center.

Examples of Rotational Symmetry

  • Square - looks the same every 90¬∞ turn
  • Equilateral triangle - looks the same every 120¬∞ turn
  • Regular hexagon - looks the same every 60¬∞ turn

Congruent and Similar Shapes

Congruent Shapes

Shapes that are exactly the same size and shape. They can be flipped, turned, or moved, but they must match perfectly when placed on top of each other.

Similar Shapes

Shapes that have the same shape but different sizes. All corresponding angles are equal, and all corresponding sides are proportional.

Example

All squares are similar to each other (same shape), but only squares with the same side length are congruent (same size).

Understanding Multi-Step Problems

Many real-life math problems require more than one operation to solve. These are called multi-step problems because you need to do several calculations in the right order.

Example: School Fundraiser

Step 1: Cookie money = 45 √ó $8 = $360

Step 2: Chocolate money = 30 √ó $12 = $360

Step 3: Total money = $360 + $360 = $720

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

When solving problems with multiple operations, we must follow the correct order:

PEMDAS Rules

  1. Parentheses first
  2. Exponents (powers)
  3. Multiplication and Division (left to right)
  4. Addition and Subtraction (left to right)

Example: 6 + 3 √ó 4 - 2

Step 1: Multiply first ‚Üí 3 √ó 4 = 12

Step 2: Now we have 6 + 12 - 2

Step 3: Add and subtract left to right ‚Üí 6 + 12 = 18

Step 4: 18 - 2 = 16

Problem-Solving Strategies

The Four-Step Process

  1. Understand: What is the problem asking? What do I know?
  2. Plan: What operations do I need? In what order?
  3. Solve: Do the calculations step by step
  4. Check: Does my answer make sense?

üßä Matter: States & Changes

Understanding Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space. Everything around you is made of matter - your desk, the air you breathe, the water you drink.

Properties of Matter

Physical Properties (can observe without changing the substance):

  • Mass: Amount of matter in an object
  • Volume: Amount of space an object takes up
  • Density: Mass per unit volume
  • Color, texture, hardness, flexibility
  • Melting point, boiling point

Chemical Properties (how matter reacts):

  • Flammability (ability to burn)
  • Reactivity with other substances
  • Ability to rust or corrode

States of Matter

1. Solids

  • Particles packed tightly together
  • Definite shape and volume
  • Particles vibrate but don't move freely
  • Examples: ice, wood, metal, rock

2. Liquids

  • Particles close but can slide past each other
  • Definite volume but no definite shape
  • Takes shape of container
  • Examples: water, oil, juice, honey

3. Gases

  • Particles far apart and move freely
  • No definite shape or volume
  • Fills entire container
  • Examples: air, oxygen, carbon dioxide, steam

Changes in Matter

Physical Changes

The substance remains the same, only its form changes. These changes are usually reversible.

  • Cutting, crushing, dissolving
  • Changes of state (melting, freezing, evaporation)
  • Mixing (without reaction)

Chemical Changes

New substances are formed. These changes are usually not reversible.

  • Burning (wood to ash and smoke)
  • Rusting (iron to iron oxide)
  • Cooking (egg proteins change)
  • Digestion (food broken down)

Two or more substances combined physically, can be separated.

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. It cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

üîß Simple Machines & Forces

Understanding Forces

A force is a push or pull that can change an object's motion, shape, or direction. Forces are measured in Newtons (N).

Types of Forces

  • Gravity: Pulls objects toward Earth's center
  • Friction: Opposes motion between surfaces
  • Magnetic: Attraction/repulsion between magnets
  • Elastic: From stretched or compressed objects
  • Applied: Direct push or pull on an object
  • Air Resistance: Opposes motion through air

Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

  • Balanced Forces: Equal forces in opposite directions = no motion change
  • Unbalanced Forces: Unequal forces = acceleration or deceleration

Simple Machines

Simple machines make work easier by changing the size or direction of a force. They help us lift heavy things, move objects, or split materials.

1. Lever

A bar that pivots on a fulcrum to lift loads.

  • First Class: Fulcrum between effort and load (seesaw, scissors)
  • Second Class: Load between fulcrum and effort (wheelbarrow, bottle opener)
  • Third Class: Effort between fulcrum and load (fishing rod, tweezers)

2. Wheel and Axle

A wheel attached to a rod that turns together.

  • Examples: doorknob, steering wheel, bicycle wheels
  • Makes it easier to move things or turn objects

3. Pulley

A wheel with a groove for a rope or cable.

  • Fixed Pulley: Changes direction of force (flagpole)
  • Movable Pulley: Reduces force needed (construction cranes)
  • Compound Pulley: Combination for maximum advantage

4. Inclined Plane

A slanted surface that helps move objects to different heights.

  • Examples: ramps, stairs, mountain roads
  • Longer ramp = less force needed but more distance

5. Wedge

Two inclined planes joined back-to-back, used for splitting.

  • Examples: knife, axe, nail, zipper
  • Concentrates force into a small area

6. Screw

An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder.

  • Examples: jar lid, drill bit, corkscrew
  • Converts rotational force to linear force

üìú Jamaica's History

The First Jamaicans: The Tainos

Before Columbus arrived, Jamaica was inhabited by the Taino (Arawak) people who called the island "Xaymaca" meaning "land of wood and water."

Taino Life and Culture

  • Villages: Lived in round houses called bohios
  • Leadership: Led by caciques (chiefs)
  • Food: Grew cassava, corn, sweet potatoes, cotton
  • Skills: Expert fishermen, made canoes, pottery
  • Religion: Worshipped zemis (spirits)
  • Games: Played batos (ball game)

Taino Legacy in Jamaica Today

  • Words: Hurricane, hammock, barbecue, canoe, tobacco
  • Foods: Bammy, pepper pot soup
  • Place names: Jamaica, Ocho Rios, Montego Bay

Spanish Period (1494-1655)

  • Columbus arrived May 5, 1494
  • Spanish settled in 1509, founded Spanish Town
  • Introduced sugarcane, citrus, cattle, horses
  • Taino population declined from disease and harsh treatment
  • Brought first enslaved Africans to Jamaica

British Period (1655-1962)

Early British Rule

  • 1655: British captured Jamaica from Spanish
  • Port Royal became "wickedest city" - pirate haven
  • 1692: Earthquake destroyed Port Royal
  • Sugar plantations expanded using enslaved African labor

Slavery and Resistance

  • Over 600,000 Africans brought to Jamaica
  • Maroons: Escaped slaves who fought for freedom
  • 1739: Peace treaty with Maroons
  • 1760: Tacky's Rebellion
  • 1831-32: Sam Sharpe Rebellion (Christmas Rebellion)
  • 1834: Slavery abolished (August 1 - Emancipation Day)
  • 1838: Full freedom (end of apprenticeship)

Path to Independence

  • 1865: Morant Bay Rebellion (Paul Bogle and George William Gordon)
  • 1938: Labor riots led by Alexander Bustamante
  • 1944: Universal adult suffrage (everyone could vote)
  • 1958-1962: West Indies Federation
  • 1962: Independence on August 6

Independent Jamaica (1962-Present)

Important Milestones

  • 1962: First Prime Minister - Alexander Bustamante
  • 1966: Queen Elizabeth II visited
  • 1966: Commonwealth Games hosted
  • 1972: Michael Manley became PM, introduced social reforms
  • 1980: Edward Seaga became PM
  • 2007: Cricket World Cup hosted
  • 2012: Celebrated 50 years of independence
  • 2022: Celebrated 60 years (Diamond Jubilee)

üèõÔ∏è Government & Democracy

Democracy in Jamaica

Jamaica is a parliamentary democracy. This means citizens elect representatives to make decisions on their behalf. Every citizen 18 years and older can vote.

Principles of Democracy

  • Rule of Law: Everyone follows the same laws
  • Equal Rights: All citizens have the same rights
  • Free Elections: People choose their leaders
  • Freedom of Speech: Express opinions freely
  • Majority Rule: Decisions by most votes
  • Minority Rights: Protection for all groups

Structure of Government

The Three Branches

1. Executive Branch
  • Governor-General: Queen's representative
  • Prime Minister: Head of government
  • Cabinet: Ministers who run ministries
  • Role: Carries out laws, runs country day-to-day
2. Legislative Branch (Parliament)
  • House of Representatives: 63 elected members (MPs)
  • Senate: 21 appointed members
  • Role: Makes laws, approves budgets
  • Location: Gordon House, Kingston
3. Judicial Branch
  • Courts: Supreme Court, Court of Appeal, Parish Courts
  • Judges: Interpret laws fairly
  • Role: Ensures justice, protects rights

How Laws Are Made

  1. Bill proposed: MP or Senator suggests new law
  2. First reading: Bill introduced to Parliament
  3. Second reading: Debate on general principles
  4. Committee stage: Detailed examination
  5. Third reading: Final debate and vote
  6. Other House: Goes through same process
  7. Governor-General: Signs bill into law

Political Parties

Major Political Parties

  • Jamaica Labour Party (JLP): Founded 1943 by Bustamante, symbol: bell
  • People's National Party (PNP): Founded 1938 by Norman Manley, symbol: head

Citizens' Rights and Responsibilities

Rights

  • Vote in elections
  • Freedom of speech and religion
  • Education and healthcare
  • Fair treatment under law
  • Own property

Responsibilities

  • Obey laws
  • Pay taxes
  • Respect others' rights
  • Protect the environment
  • Participate in democracy
  • Jury duty when called

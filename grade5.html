Types of Fractions

Proper Fractions

The numerator is smaller than the denominator (less than 1 whole).

Examples: 1/2, 3/4, 5/8, 7/10

Improper Fractions

The numerator is equal to or greater than the denominator (equal to or greater than 1 whole).

Examples: 5/4, 8/3, 9/9, 15/7

Mixed Numbers

A whole number combined with a proper fraction.

Examples: 2 1/3, 4 2/5, 1 3/4

Converting Between Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers

Converting Improper Fractions to Mixed Numbers

  1. Divide the numerator by the denominator
  2. The quotient becomes the whole number
  3. The remainder becomes the new numerator
  4. Keep the same denominator

Example: Convert 17/5 to a Mixed Number

17 √∑ 5 = 3 remainder 2

So 17/5 = 3 2/5

Converting Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions

  1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator
  2. Add the numerator to this product
  3. Write the sum over the original denominator

Example: Convert 4 3/7 to an Improper Fraction

(4 √ó 7) + 3 = 28 + 3 = 31

So 4 3/7 = 31/7

Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Same Denominators

When fractions have the same denominator, add or subtract the numerators and keep the same denominator.

Examples:

2/7 + 3/7 = 5/7

5/9 - 2/9 = 3/9 = 1/3

Different Denominators

When fractions have different denominators, you need to find a common denominator first.

  1. Find the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the denominators
  2. Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions with the common denominator
  3. Add or subtract the numerators
  4. Simplify if possible

Example: 1/4 + 1/6

LCM of 4 and 6 = 12

1/4 = 3/12 and 1/6 = 2/12

3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12

Multiplying Fractions

To multiply fractions, multiply the numerators together and multiply the denominators together.

Example: 2/3 √ó 4/5

(2 √ó 4)/(3 √ó 5) = 8/15

Dividing Fractions

To divide fractions, multiply by the reciprocal (flip the second fraction).

Example: 3/4 √∑ 2/5

3/4 √ó 5/2 = 15/8 = 1 7/8

Symmetry

A shape has symmetry if it looks exactly the same after being flipped, turned, or moved in a certain way.

Line Symmetry (Reflection Symmetry)

A shape has line symmetry if it can be folded along a line so that both halves match exactly.

Examples of Line Symmetry

  • Butterfly - 1 line of symmetry (vertical)
  • Square - 4 lines of symmetry
  • Circle - infinite lines of symmetry
  • Rectangle - 2 lines of symmetry

Rotational Symmetry

A shape has rotational symmetry if it looks the same after being turned (rotated) by less than a full turn around its center.

Examples of Rotational Symmetry

  • Square - looks the same every 90¬∞ turn
  • Equilateral triangle - looks the same every 120¬∞ turn
  • Regular hexagon - looks the same every 60¬∞ turn

Congruent and Similar Shapes

Congruent Shapes

Shapes that are exactly the same size and shape. They can be flipped, turned, or moved, but they must match perfectly when placed on top of each other.

Similar Shapes

Shapes that have the same shape but different sizes. All corresponding angles are equal, and all corresponding sides are proportional.

Example

All squares are similar to each other (same shape), but only squares with the same side length are congruent (same size).

üßä Matter: States & Changes

Understanding Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space. Everything around you is made of matter - your desk, the air you breathe, the water you drink.

Properties of Matter

Physical Properties (can observe without changing the substance):

  • Mass: Amount of matter in an object
  • Volume: Amount of space an object takes up
  • Density: Mass per unit volume
  • Color, texture, hardness, flexibility
  • Melting point, boiling point

Chemical Properties (how matter reacts):

  • Flammability (ability to burn)
  • Reactivity with other substances
  • Ability to rust or corrode

States of Matter

1. Solids

  • Particles packed tightly together
  • Definite shape and volume
  • Particles vibrate but don't move freely
  • Examples: ice, wood, metal, rock

2. Liquids

  • Particles close but can slide past each other
  • Definite volume but no definite shape
  • Takes shape of container
  • Examples: water, oil, juice, honey

3. Gases

  • Particles far apart and move freely
  • No definite shape or volume
  • Fills entire container
  • Examples: air, oxygen, carbon dioxide, steam

Changes in Matter

Physical Changes

The substance remains the same, only its form changes. These changes are usually reversible.

  • Cutting, crushing, dissolving
  • Changes of state (melting, freezing, evaporation)
  • Mixing (without reaction)

Chemical Changes

New substances are formed. These changes are usually not reversible.

  • Burning (wood to ash and smoke)
  • Rusting (iron to iron oxide)
  • Cooking (egg proteins change)
  • Digestion (food broken down)

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. It cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

Noun Phrases

A noun phrase includes a noun and all words that modify it:

Subject-Verb Agreement

Rules to Remember
  • Singular subjects need singular verbs: The girl runs fast.
  • Plural subjects need plural verbs: The girls run fast.
  • Collective nouns (team, class, family) are usually singular: The team is winning.
  • Either/or: Verb agrees with closer subject: Either the cat or the dogs are outside.
  • Everyone/everybody is always singular: Everyone has a book.

Pronouns and Agreement

Pronouns must agree with their antecedents (the words they replace) in number and gender.

Person Singular Plural
First Person I, me, my, mine we, us, our, ours
Second Person you, your, yours you, your, yours
Third Person he, she, it, him, her, his, hers, its they, them, their, theirs

Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

  • ‚úì The boy lost his book. (singular matches singular)
  • ‚úì The girls found their bags. (plural matches plural)
  • ‚úó Everyone should bring their lunch. (incorrect - use "his or her")

Erosion and Deposition

How Erosion Works

Erosion is when soil and rocks are worn away and moved by water, wind, or ice.

  • Water erosion: Rain and rivers wash away soil
  • Wind erosion: Strong winds blow away loose soil
  • Coastal erosion: Waves wear away the coastline

How Deposition Works

Deposition is when eroded materials are dropped in new places.

  • River deltas: Rivers drop soil at their mouths
  • Beaches: Waves deposit sand along the coast
  • Fertile valleys: Rivers deposit rich soil in valleys

Human Activities That Cause Erosion

Activities That Increase Erosion

  • Deforestation: Cutting down trees removes protection for soil
  • Poor farming: Not rotating crops wears out soil
  • Overgrazing: Too many animals eat all the grass
  • Construction: Building without proper planning
  • Mining: Removing vegetation and topsoil

Methods to Reduce Erosion

  • Plant trees: Tree roots hold soil in place
  • Terracing: Create steps on hillsides for farming
  • Cover crops: Plant crops that protect soil
  • Contour farming: Farm across slopes, not up and down
  • Build barriers: Use stones or plants to slow water flow

Importance of Wetlands and Forests

Wetlands and forests are very important for Jamaica's environment and for people. They provide many benefits that help keep our island healthy and beautiful.

Uses and Importance of Wetlands

What are Wetlands?

Wetlands are areas where water covers the soil for most of the year. They include swamps, marshes, and mangroves.

Benefits of Wetlands
  • Water filter: Clean pollution from water before it reaches the sea
  • Flood protection: Soak up extra water during heavy rains
  • Wildlife homes: Provide safe places for birds, fish, and other animals
  • Coastal protection: Mangroves protect coastlines from storms
  • Fish nurseries: Many fish grow up in wetlands before going to sea
  • Tourism: People visit to see birds and enjoy nature
Jamaica's Important Wetlands
  • Black River Morass: Largest wetland, home to crocodiles
  • Negril Morass: Important bird habitat
  • Palisadoes-Port Royal: Mangrove forests
  • Portland Bight: Protected area with mangroves

Uses and Importance of Forests

Benefits of Forests
  • Clean air: Trees take in carbon dioxide and give out oxygen
  • Prevent erosion: Tree roots hold soil in place
  • Regulate water: Forests control how water flows in rivers
  • Wildlife habitat: Home to many animals and plants
  • Medicine: Many plants in forests are used for medicine
  • Wood products: Lumber for building, paper, furniture
  • Recreation: Places for hiking, camping, and enjoying nature
  • Climate control: Keep areas cooler and more comfortable
Jamaica's Forest Types
  • Rainforest: Blue Mountains, lots of rain and mist
  • Dry forest: South coast areas with less rain
  • Mangrove forests: Along coastlines in salt water
  • Mountain forests: Cool, cloudy areas in highlands

Negative Impact of Human Activities

Unfortunately, human activities sometimes harm our forests and wetlands. It's important to understand these problems so we can help solve them.

How Humans Damage Forests and Wetlands

Deforestation
  • Cutting down trees for farmland
  • Building houses and roads
  • Taking wood for charcoal
  • Results in soil erosion and flooding
Pollution
  • Throwing garbage in water
  • Chemical from farms running into rivers
  • Oil spills from boats
  • Makes water unsafe for animals and people
Overfishing
  • Catching too many fish
  • Using nets that hurt coral reefs
  • Disturbing fish breeding areas
  • Leads to fewer fish for the future
Development
  • Building hotels too close to beaches
  • Filling in wetlands for construction
  • Removing mangroves
  • Destroys animal homes and natural protection

Solutions to Reduce Environmental Damage

Ways to Protect Forests and Wetlands

  • Reforestation: Plant new trees to replace ones that were cut
  • Protected areas: Create national parks where no cutting is allowed
  • Sustainable logging: Cut trees carefully and plant new ones
  • Education: Teach people why forests are important
  • Alternative income: Help people find other ways to make money besides cutting trees
  • Ecotourism: Make money by showing visitors natural areas
  • Reduce, reuse, recycle: Use less paper and wood products

Water Pollution: Causes and Solutions

Causes of Water Pollution

Evidence of Water Pollution

You can identify polluted water by looking for:

  • Strange colors: Water that is not clear (brown, green, oily)
  • Bad smells: Foul odors from the water
  • Dead fish: Fish floating on the surface
  • Garbage: Plastic, bottles, and other trash in the water
  • Foam: Unusual bubbles or foam on the surface
  • No wildlife: Areas where no birds or fish are seen

What We Can Do

  • Proper waste disposal: Put garbage in bins, not in rivers
  • Use less chemicals: Reduce household cleaners and pesticides

  • Educate others: Teach friends and family about water protection

  • Water treatment plants: Clean sewage before it reaches rivers
  • Laws and regulations: Rules to prevent pollution
  • Monitoring: Regular testing of water quality
  • Protected watersheds: Keep development away from water sources
  • Public education: Campaigns to raise awareness

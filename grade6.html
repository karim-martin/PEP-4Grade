ü´Ä Human Body Systems

The Digestive System

The digestive system breaks down food into nutrients that your body can use for energy, growth, and repair. It's like a long factory assembly line that processes everything you eat.

Parts of the Digestive System

  • Mouth: Where digestion begins. Teeth break food into smaller pieces, and saliva starts breaking down starches.
  • Esophagus: A muscular tube that pushes food from your mouth to your stomach using wave-like contractions.
  • Stomach: A stretchy bag that churns food and mixes it with acid and enzymes to break it down further.
  • Small Intestine: Where most nutrients are absorbed into the bloodstream. It's about 6 meters long!
  • Large Intestine: Absorbs water and forms waste products (feces) for elimination.
  • Liver: Produces bile to help digest fats and filters toxins from blood.
  • Pancreas: Makes digestive enzymes and hormones like insulin.
Amazing Fact: Your stomach can hold up to 4 liters of food and liquid! That's like two large bottles of soda. The stomach acid is so strong it could dissolve metal, but a special mucus lining protects your stomach walls.

The Digestive Process

  1. Ingestion: Taking food into the mouth
  2. Mechanical Digestion: Physical breakdown by teeth and stomach churning
  3. Chemical Digestion: Breaking down food molecules using enzymes
  4. Absorption: Nutrients enter the bloodstream through intestinal walls
  5. Elimination: Removing waste products from the body

The Respiratory System

The respiratory system brings oxygen into your body and removes carbon dioxide. Every cell in your body needs oxygen to produce energy.

Parts of the Respiratory System

  • Nose and Mouth: Entry points for air. The nose filters, warms, and moistens air.
  • Trachea (Windpipe): A tube lined with tiny hairs (cilia) that trap dust and germs.
  • Bronchi: Two tubes that branch off from the trachea into each lung.
  • Bronchioles: Smaller tubes that branch throughout the lungs like tree branches.
  • Alveoli: Tiny air sacs where oxygen and carbon dioxide are exchanged with blood.
  • Diaphragm: A large muscle that helps you breathe by moving up and down.

ü´Å Breathing Investigation

What to do: Count how many breaths you take in one minute while resting. Then do 20 jumping jacks and count again.

What happens: Your breathing rate increases because your muscles need more oxygen during exercise.

Why: When you exercise, your cells work harder and need more oxygen to produce energy. Your respiratory system responds by breathing faster.

The Circulatory System

The circulatory system transports blood, nutrients, oxygen, and waste products throughout your body. It's like a highway system with the heart as the central pump.

Parts of the Circulatory System

  • Heart: A muscular pump with four chambers that pushes blood through your body.
  • Arteries: Blood vessels that carry oxygen-rich blood away from the heart.
  • Veins: Blood vessels that carry oxygen-poor blood back to the heart.
  • Capillaries: Tiny blood vessels where oxygen and nutrients are exchanged with body tissues.
  • Blood: The fluid that carries oxygen, nutrients, and waste products.
Heart Facts: Your heart beats about 100,000 times per day! That's about 35 million beats per year. If you laid out all your blood vessels end to end, they would circle the Earth more than twice.

What Blood Contains

  • Red Blood Cells: Carry oxygen from lungs to body tissues
  • White Blood Cells: Fight infections and diseases
  • Platelets: Help blood clot when you get a cut
  • Plasma: The liquid part that carries nutrients and waste

The Nervous System

The nervous system controls all body functions and allows you to think, feel, and move. It's like your body's electrical wiring system.

Parts of the Nervous System

  • Brain: The control center that processes information and makes decisions.
  • Spinal Cord: A bundle of nerves that carries messages between the brain and body.
  • Nerves: Pathways that carry electrical signals throughout the body.
  • Sensory Organs: Eyes, ears, nose, tongue, and skin that detect information from the environment.

How Your Brain Works

  • Cerebrum: Controls thinking, memory, and voluntary movements
  • Cerebellum: Controls balance and coordination
  • Brain Stem: Controls automatic functions like breathing and heartbeat

‚ö° Electricity and Circuits

What is Electricity?

Electricity is a form of energy caused by the movement of tiny particles called electrons. It powers almost everything we use daily, from lights to computers to refrigerators.

Types of Electricity

  • Static Electricity: Build-up of electric charges on surfaces (like when you rub a balloon on your hair)
  • Current Electricity: Flow of electric charges through a conductor (like electricity in wires)

‚ö° Static Electricity Experiment

Materials: Balloon, small pieces of paper, wool cloth

Steps:

  1. Rub the balloon on wool cloth for 30 seconds
  2. Hold the balloon near the paper pieces
  3. Watch what happens!

Result: The paper jumps toward the balloon because rubbing creates static electric charges.

Electric Circuits

An electric circuit is a complete path that allows electricity to flow from a power source, through wires and components, and back to the source.

Circuit Components

  • Power Source: Battery or electrical outlet that provides energy
  • Wires: Conductors that carry electricity
  • Load: Device that uses electricity (light bulb, motor, etc.)
  • Switch: Device that opens or closes the circuit

Types of Circuits

Series Circuit

Components connected in a single path. If one component breaks, the whole circuit stops working.

Example: Old Christmas lights where if one bulb burns out, all lights go off.

Parallel Circuit

Components connected in separate branches. If one component breaks, others keep working.

Example: House wiring where each room can be controlled separately.

Conductors and Insulators

Materials can be classified by how well they allow electricity to pass through them.

Conductors (Allow electricity to flow)

  • Metals like copper, aluminum, gold, silver
  • Salt water
  • Graphite (pencil lead)
  • Human body (contains water and salts)

Insulators (Block electricity flow)

  • Rubber
  • Plastic
  • Glass
  • Wood (when dry)
  • Air

Electrical Safety

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Rules

  • Never touch electrical outlets with wet hands
  • Don't use electrical appliances near water
  • Unplug appliances by pulling the plug, not the cord
  • Never put metal objects in electrical outlets
  • Stay away from power lines, especially during storms
  • Tell an adult if you see damaged electrical cords

Why Electricity is Dangerous

Electric current can cause:

  • Burns to skin and internal organs
  • Muscle contractions that prevent letting go
  • Heart rhythm problems
  • Fires from overheating

Electrical Energy in Daily Life

How We Use Electricity

  • Lighting: Converting electrical energy to light energy
  • Heating: Electric stoves, water heaters, space heaters
  • Motion: Fans, washing machines, blenders
  • Communication: Phones, computers, televisions
  • Cooling: Air conditioners, refrigerators

üåü Light and Sound

Properties of Light

Light is a form of energy that allows us to see. It travels in straight lines at incredible speed and has many fascinating properties.

How Light Behaves

  • Travels in straight lines: Light rays move in straight paths unless something blocks or bends them
  • Reflects: Bounces off surfaces like mirrors
  • Refracts: Bends when passing through different materials
  • Absorbs: Gets soaked up by dark or rough surfaces

üî¶ Shadow Investigation

Materials: Flashlight, various objects, white wall

Steps:

  1. Shine flashlight on different objects
  2. Move objects closer and farther from light
  3. Observe shadow sizes and sharpness

What you'll discover: Objects closer to light make bigger, fuzzier shadows. Objects farther away make smaller, sharper shadows.

Types of Materials and Light

Transparent

Allow light to pass through clearly

Examples: Clear glass, water, air

Translucent

Allow some light through but scatter it

Examples: Frosted glass, wax paper

Opaque

Block light completely

Examples: Wood, metal, your body

Color and Light

White light is actually made up of all colors mixed together. We can separate white light into a rainbow using a prism.

The Color Spectrum

Colors in order: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet (Remember: ROY G. BIV)

How We See Colors

  • Red objects: Absorb all colors except red, which they reflect to our eyes
  • Black objects: Absorb all colors of light
  • White objects: Reflect all colors of light
  • Green plants: Absorb red and blue light for photosynthesis, reflect green
Rainbow Science: Rainbows form when sunlight shines through water droplets in the air. The water drops act like tiny prisms, separating white light into all its colors. You can make your own rainbow with a garden hose on a sunny day!

Properties of Sound

Sound is a form of energy created by vibrations. These vibrations travel through air, water, and solid materials as waves.

How Sound is Made

  • Vibration: All sounds start with something moving back and forth rapidly
  • Sound Waves: Vibrations push air molecules, creating waves that travel to our ears
  • Speed: Sound travels about 343 meters per second in air (much slower than light)

üéµ Sound Vibration Experiment

Materials: Rubber band, ruler, rice grains, speaker

Activity 1: Stretch rubber band and pluck it. Notice the vibration and sound.

Activity 2: Sprinkle rice on a speaker playing music. Watch the rice dance!

What it shows: Sound is created by vibrations that we can see and feel.

Properties of Sound Waves

  • Pitch: How high or low a sound is (depends on frequency of vibrations)
  • Volume: How loud or soft a sound is (depends on amplitude of vibrations)
  • Echo: Sound waves bouncing back from surfaces
  • Absorption: Soft materials soak up sound waves

How We Hear Sound

Parts of the Ear

  1. Outer Ear: Collects sound waves like a funnel
  2. Middle Ear: Tiny bones amplify vibrations
  3. Inner Ear: Converts vibrations into nerve signals sent to the brain

Protecting Your Hearing

üîá Hearing Safety

  • Keep music volume at safe levels (no more than 60% of maximum)
  • Wear ear protection around loud machinery
  • Take breaks from loud environments
  • Never put objects in your ears

Sound in Different Materials

Sound travels at different speeds through different materials:

  • Fastest: Solids (like metal railway tracks)
  • Medium: Liquids (like water)
  • Slowest: Gases (like air)
  • No sound: Vacuum (outer space has no sound because there's no air to carry sound waves)

PEP Exam Tip

Remember: Light travels much faster than sound. That's why you see lightning before hearing thunder! Count seconds between lightning and thunder, then divide by 3 to estimate distance in kilometers.

What is a Force?

A force is a push or pull that can change the motion of an object. Forces are all around us and affect everything we do, from walking to throwing a ball.

Types of Forces

Contact Forces

Forces that require touching

  • Push (shoving a box)
  • Pull (tugging a rope)
  • Friction (resistance to motion)
  • Air resistance

Non-Contact Forces

Forces that work at a distance

  • Gravity (pulls objects down)
  • Magnetism (attracts metals)
  • Static electricity

Gravity

Gravity is the force that pulls all objects toward the center of the Earth. It's what keeps us from floating away and makes things fall when dropped.

Effects of Gravity

  • Gives weight to objects
  • Makes objects fall toward Earth
  • Keeps atmosphere around Earth
  • Creates tides in oceans
  • Keeps planets in orbit around the sun

‚öñÔ∏è Gravity Investigation

Materials: Various objects (feather, coin, paper, rock)

Prediction: Which will fall faster?

Test: Drop objects from the same height simultaneously

Result: Without air resistance, all objects fall at the same rate! Air resistance affects light objects more than heavy ones.

Friction

Friction is a force that opposes motion when two surfaces rub against each other. It can be helpful or problematic depending on the situation.

Helpful Friction

  • Allows walking without slipping
  • Helps car tires grip the road
  • Enables brakes to stop vehicles
  • Lets you hold objects in your hands

Unwanted Friction

  • Wears out car tires and brake pads
  • Makes machines less efficient
  • Creates heat that can damage parts
  • Makes it harder to move heavy objects

Reducing Friction

  • Lubricants: Oil, grease, or soap make surfaces slippery
  • Wheels: Rolling reduces friction compared to sliding
  • Smooth surfaces: Less rough surfaces create less friction
  • Air cushions: Hovercrafts float on air to eliminate surface contact

Motion and Speed

Motion is when an object changes position over time. Speed measures how fast this change happens.

Types of Motion

  • Linear Motion: Moving in a straight line (car on highway)
  • Circular Motion: Moving in a circle (wheel spinning)
  • Oscillatory Motion: Moving back and forth (pendulum)
  • Random Motion: Moving unpredictably (bee flying)

Measuring Speed

Speed = Distance √∑ Time

Example: If you walk 100 meters in 50 seconds:

Speed = 100m √∑ 50s = 2 meters per second

Newton's Laws of Motion

First Law (Law of Inertia)

Objects at rest stay at rest, and objects in motion stay in motion, unless acted upon by an outside force.

Example: When a car stops suddenly, passengers continue moving forward due to inertia. Seatbelts provide the force needed to stop them safely.

Second Law (Force and Acceleration)

The bigger the force applied to an object, the greater its acceleration. Heavier objects need more force to accelerate.

Example: It takes more force to push a full shopping cart than an empty one to achieve the same acceleration.

Third Law (Action-Reaction)

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Example: When you walk, you push backward on the ground, and the ground pushes forward on you. When you jump, you push down on Earth, and Earth pushes up on you.

Simple Machines

Simple machines make work easier by changing the direction or amount of force needed.

Six Types of Simple Machines

Lever

A rigid bar that pivots on a fulcrum

Examples: Seesaw, scissors, bottle opener

Wheel and Axle

A wheel connected to a central axle

Examples: Car wheels, doorknobs, steering wheels

Pulley

A rope over a wheel to lift objects

Examples: Flag poles, window blinds, cranes

Inclined Plane

A slanted surface to move objects up

Examples: Ramps, stairs, mountain roads

Wedge

Two inclined planes back to back

Examples: Knives, axes, nails, teeth

Screw

An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder

Examples: Screws, bolts, jar lids

üåø Ecosystems and Biodiversity

What is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with each other and their non-living environment in a specific area.

Components of an Ecosystem

  • Biotic factors: All living things (plants, animals, bacteria, fungi)
  • Abiotic factors: Non-living things (sunlight, water, soil, air, temperature)

Food Chains and Food Webs

Energy flows through ecosystems from producers to consumers in predictable patterns.

Levels in a Food Chain

  1. Producers (Plants): Make their own food using sunlight
  2. Primary Consumers: Herbivores that eat plants
  3. Secondary Consumers: Carnivores that eat herbivores
  4. Tertiary Consumers: Top predators
  5. Decomposers: Break down dead material and recycle nutrients

Caribbean Food Chain Example

If one link is removed, the entire chain is affected!

Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life in ecosystems, including the diversity of species, genes, and ecosystems themselves.

Why Biodiversity Matters

  • Provides food, medicine, and materials
  • Helps ecosystems recover from disturbances
  • Creates more stable food webs
  • Supports ecosystem services like pollination

Caribbean Ecosystems

Coral Reefs

Underwater ecosystems with high biodiversity

Key species: Coral, tropical fish, sea turtles

Mangrove Forests

Coastal wetlands that protect shorelines

Key species: Mangrove trees, crabs, birds

Rainforests

Dense forests with many layers of life

Key species: Various trees, insects, birds

üå°Ô∏è Climate Change and Environmental Impact

Understanding Climate vs Weather

  • Weather: Short-term atmospheric conditions (today it's sunny)
  • Climate: Long-term weather patterns (Jamaica is tropical)

Causes of Climate Change

Greenhouse Effect

Natural process where certain gases trap heat in Earth's atmosphere, but human activities are increasing these gases.

Major Greenhouse Gases

  • Carbon dioxide: From burning fossil fuels
  • Methane: From agriculture and waste
  • Nitrous oxide: From fertilizers and industry

Effects on Caribbean Countries

  • Rising sea levels threatening coastal areas
  • More intense hurricanes
  • Coral bleaching from warmer oceans
  • Changes in rainfall patterns affecting agriculture

Environmental Solutions

Individual Actions

  • Reduce, reuse, recycle materials
  • Use energy-efficient appliances
  • Walk, bike, or use public transportation
  • Plant trees and protect forests
  • Use renewable energy when possible

Jamaica's Environmental Challenges

Jamaica faces unique challenges including deforestation, pollution of rivers and harbors, loss of biodiversity, and vulnerability to extreme weather events. Understanding these issues helps us become better environmental stewards.

üî¨ Scientific Investigation Skills

Planning an Investigation

Steps to Follow

  1. Observation: Notice something interesting or puzzling
  2. Question: Form a testable question
  3. Hypothesis: Make an educated prediction
  4. Materials: List what you need for testing
  5. Procedure: Plan step-by-step instructions
  6. Variables: Identify what changes and what stays the same
  7. Results: Record observations and measurements
  8. Conclusion: Explain what you learned

Variables in Experiments

  • Independent Variable: What you change on purpose
  • Dependent Variable: What you measure or observe
  • Controlled Variables: What you keep the same

Example Investigation: Plant Growth

Question: Does the amount of water affect how tall plants grow?

Independent Variable: Amount of water given

Dependent Variable: Plant height

Controlled Variables: Type of plant, soil, light, temperature

Recording and Presenting Data

Ways to Show Data

  • Tables: Organize numbers in rows and columns
  • Bar graphs: Compare different categories
  • Line graphs: Show changes over time
  • Pie charts: Show parts of a whole

Writing Scientific Reports

  • Use clear, simple language
  • Include labeled diagrams
  • Report what actually happened, not what you wanted
  • Suggest improvements for future investigations

PEP Exam Tip

Practice identifying variables in different scenarios. Remember that good investigations only change one thing at a time (independent variable) while keeping everything else the same.

üß™ Chemistry Basics: Solutions and Mixtures

States of Matter

All matter exists in three main states, each with different properties.

Solids

Particles tightly packed, definite shape and volume

Examples: Ice, rocks, wood

Liquids

Particles loosely connected, definite volume but take shape of container

Examples: Water, oil, juice

Gases

Particles spread out, no definite shape or volume

Examples: Air, steam, helium

Mixtures

A mixture contains two or more substances that can be separated by physical means.

Types of Mixtures

  • Homogeneous: Evenly mixed, looks the same throughout (salt water)
  • Heterogeneous: Unevenly mixed, can see different parts (oil and water)

Separating Mixtures

  • Filtering: Separate solids from liquids (sand from water)
  • Evaporation: Remove liquid to leave solid behind (salt from salt water)
  • Magnetic separation: Use magnets to remove magnetic materials
  • Sieving: Separate by particle size

Solutions

A solution is a special type of homogeneous mixture where one substance (solute) dissolves completely in another (solvent).

Parts of a Solution

  • Solvent: The substance that does the dissolving (usually water)
  • Solute: The substance being dissolved (like sugar or salt)

üß™ Solution Investigation

Materials: Water, sugar, salt, oil, food coloring

Test: Try dissolving each substance in water

Observe: Which substances form clear solutions?

Result: Sugar, salt, and food coloring dissolve; oil does not.

Factors Affecting Dissolving

  • Temperature: Hot water usually dissolves more than cold water
  • Stirring: Mixing helps substances dissolve faster
  • Particle size: Smaller particles dissolve faster
  • Amount of solvent: More water can dissolve more solute

Acids and Bases

Acids and bases are special solutions with different properties.

Common Acids

  • Lemon juice (citric acid)
  • Vinegar (acetic acid)
  • Stomach acid (hydrochloric acid)

Common Bases

  • Soap
  • Baking soda
  • Antacid tablets

Testing Acids and Bases

pH indicators like litmus paper change color:

  • Red litmus turns blue: Base present
  • Blue litmus turns red: Acid present
  • No color change: Neutral (like pure water)

üî¢ Algebra: Expressions and Equations

What is Algebra?

Algebra uses letters and symbols to represent unknown numbers. It helps us solve problems when we don't know all the numbers involved.

Understanding Variables

A variable is a letter that stands for an unknown number. Common variables are x, y, n, and a.

Examples of Variables

  • If n = 5, then n + 3 = 5 + 3 = 8
  • If x = 10, then 2x = 2 √ó 10 = 20
  • If y = 7, then y - 4 = 7 - 4 = 3

Algebraic Expressions

An algebraic expression contains numbers, variables, and operation signs (+, -, √ó, √∑).

Parts of an Expression

  • Terms: Parts separated by + or - signs
  • Coefficients: Numbers multiplied by variables
  • Constants: Numbers without variables

Expression: 3x + 5y - 7

  • Terms: 3x, 5y, -7
  • Coefficients: 3 (for x), 5 (for y)
  • Constant: -7

Substitution

Substitution means replacing variables with given numbers to find the value of an expression.

Example: Evaluate 2a + 3b when a = 4 and b = 6

Step 1: Substitute values: 2(4) + 3(6)

Step 2: Multiply: 8 + 18

Step 3: Add: 26

Simple Equations

An equation shows that two expressions are equal. We solve equations to find the value of the variable.

One-Step Equations

Example 1: Solve x + 7 = 12

Subtract 7 from both sides:

x + 7 - 7 = 12 - 7

x = 5

Example 2: Solve 3y = 15

Divide both sides by 3:

3y √∑ 3 = 15 √∑ 3

y = 5

Two-Step Equations

Example: Solve 2x + 5 = 13

Step 1: Subtract 5 from both sides

2x + 5 - 5 = 13 - 5

2x = 8

Step 2: Divide both sides by 2

2x √∑ 2 = 8 √∑ 2

x = 4

Writing Equations from Word Problems

Key Words and Phrases

  • Addition: sum, total, plus, increased by, more than
  • Subtraction: difference, minus, decreased by, less than
  • Multiplication: product, times, of, multiplied by
  • Division: quotient, divided by, per, ratio
  • Equals: is, equals, is equal to, results in

Word Problem Example

Problem: "Maria has 3 more stickers than twice the number John has. If Maria has 15 stickers, how many does John have?"

Let: j = number of John's stickers

Equation: 2j + 3 = 15

Solution: 2j = 12, so j = 6

Answer: John has 6 stickers

PEP Exam Tip

Always check your answer by substituting it back into the original equation. If both sides are equal, your answer is correct!

‚öñÔ∏è Ratios and Proportions

Understanding Ratios

A ratio compares two or more quantities. It shows the relationship between different amounts.

Writing Ratios

Ratios can be written in three ways:

  • Using words: "3 to 4"
  • Using a colon: "3:4"
  • As a fraction: "3/4"

Example: Class Ratio

In a class of 30 students, 12 are boys and 18 are girls.

Ratio of boys to girls: 12:18 = 2:3 (simplified)

Ratio of girls to total students: 18:30 = 3:5

Ratio of boys to total students: 12:30 = 2:5

Simplifying Ratios

To simplify a ratio, divide all numbers by their greatest common factor (GCF).

Simplifying Example

Ratio: 15:20

GCF of 15 and 20: 5

Simplified: 15√∑5 : 20√∑5 = 3:4

Equivalent Ratios

Equivalent ratios represent the same relationship. They are created by multiplying or dividing both parts by the same number.

Equivalent Ratios

2:3 = 4:6 = 6:9 = 8:12

All these ratios are equivalent because they represent the same relationship.

Unit Rates

A unit rate compares a quantity to 1 unit of another quantity.

Unit Rate Examples

  • Speed: 60 kilometers per hour (60 km per 1 hour)
  • Price: $3 per kilogram ($3 for 1 kilogram)
  • Productivity: 25 problems per minute (25 problems in 1 minute)

Finding Unit Rates

Example: Finding Cost per Item

Problem: 6 apples cost $12. What is the cost per apple?

Solution: $12 √∑ 6 apples = $2 per apple

Proportions

A proportion states that two ratios are equal. It can be written as a/b = c/d.

Cross Multiplication

To solve proportions, use cross multiplication: If a/b = c/d, then a√ód = b√óc

Solving Proportions

Problem: 3/4 = x/12

Cross multiply: 3 √ó 12 = 4 √ó x

Simplify: 36 = 4x

Solve: x = 36 √∑ 4 = 9

Real-World Applications

Recipe Problems

Recipe Scaling

Problem: A recipe for 4 people needs 2 cups of flour. How much flour is needed for 6 people?

Set up proportion: 2 cups/4 people = x cups/6 people

Cross multiply: 2 √ó 6 = 4 √ó x

Solve: 12 = 4x, so x = 3 cups

Map and Scale Problems

Map Scale

Problem: On a map, 2 cm represents 5 km. If two cities are 8 cm apart on the map, what is their actual distance?

Set up proportion: 2 cm/5 km = 8 cm/x km

Cross multiply: 2 √ó x = 5 √ó 8

Solve: 2x = 40, so x = 20 km

PEP Exam Tip

When setting up proportions, make sure the units match up correctly. Put similar quantities in the same positions (numerators together, denominators together).

  2. If 3 notebooks cost $9, what is the cost per notebook?
  3. Solve the proportion: 5/8 = x/24

Collecting and Organizing Data

Statistics helps us collect, organize, and analyze information to make decisions and predictions.

Types of Data

  • Qualitative: Non-numerical data (colors, names, categories)
  • Quantitative: Numerical data (heights, weights, test scores)

Ways to Collect Data

  • Surveys: Asking questions to gather information
  • Observations: Recording what you see
  • Experiments: Testing to get results
  • Research: Using existing information

Measures of Central Tendency

These measures help us find the "center" or typical value of a data set.

Mean (Average)

Add all values and divide by the number of values

Test scores: 85, 92, 78, 90, 85

Mean: (85 + 92 + 78 + 90 + 85) √∑ 5 = 430 √∑ 5 = 86

Median

The middle value when data is arranged in order

Ordered scores: 78, 85, 85, 90, 92

Median: 85 (middle value)

Mode

The value that appears most often

Scores: 78, 85, 85, 90, 92

Mode: 85 (appears twice)

  • Line Graph: Shows changes over time
  • Pie Chart: Shows parts of a whole

Probability

Probability measures the chance that something will happen. It ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

Probability Formula

Probability = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes

Coin Flip Example

Question: What's the probability of getting heads?

Favorable outcomes: 1 (heads)

Total outcomes: 2 (heads or tails)

Probability: 1/2 = 0.5 = 50%

Dice Roll Example

Question: What's the probability of rolling a number greater than 4?

Favorable outcomes: 2 (rolling 5 or 6)

Total outcomes: 6 (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6)

Probability: 2/6 = 1/3

Probability Expressions

  • Impossible: Probability = 0
  • Unlikely: Probability less than 1/2
  • Equally likely: Probability = 1/2
  • Likely: Probability greater than 1/2
  • Certain: Probability = 1

PEP Exam Tip

When calculating mean, median, and mode, be careful with the data. Make sure to order values for median and count frequencies for mode. For probability, always check that your answer is between 0 and 1.

üìñ Critical Reading and Analysis

What is Critical Reading?

Critical reading means carefully analyzing what you read, questioning the author's ideas, and forming your own opinions based on evidence.

Active Reading Strategies

  • Preview: Look at titles, headings, and pictures before reading
  • Predict: Guess what the text might be about
  • Question: Ask yourself questions as you read
  • Connect: Relate the text to your own experiences
  • Visualize: Create mental pictures of what you're reading
  • Summarize: Retell the main ideas in your own words

Finding Main Ideas and Supporting Details

Main Ideas

  • The most important point the author makes
  • Often stated in the topic sentence of a paragraph
  • Sometimes implied and you must infer it

Supporting Details

  • Facts, examples, or explanations that support the main idea
  • Help prove or explain the main point
  • Can be statistics, quotes, anecdotes, or descriptions

Example Paragraph

Main Idea: "Coral reefs are in danger around the world."

Supporting Details:

  • Ocean temperatures are rising due to climate change
  • Pollution from land is affecting water quality
  • Overfishing disrupts the ecosystem balance
  • Scientists estimate 50% of coral reefs have been lost

Making Inferences

An inference is an educated guess based on evidence from the text plus your background knowledge.

How to Make Inferences

  1. Look for clues in the text
  2. Think about what you already know
  3. Combine text evidence with background knowledge
  4. Make a logical conclusion

Inference Example

Text: "Maria grabbed her umbrella and rushed out the door, avoiding the puddles on the sidewalk."

Clues: Umbrella, avoiding puddles

Background knowledge: People use umbrellas when it rains

Inference: It is raining outside

Analyzing Author's Purpose

Three Main Purposes

  • Inform: To teach or give information (textbooks, news articles)
  • Persuade: To convince readers to think or act a certain way (advertisements, opinion pieces)
  • Entertain: To amuse or tell a story (novels, poems, jokes)

Identifying Author's Purpose

  • Look at the type of text
  • Notice the language used (emotional, factual, humorous)
  • Consider the intended audience
  • Think about what action the author wants you to take

Evaluating Evidence and Arguments

Types of Evidence

  • Facts: Information that can be proven true
  • Statistics: Numerical data that supports claims
  • Expert opinions: Views from knowledgeable people
  • Examples: Specific cases that illustrate points
  • Anecdotes: Personal stories or experiences

Questions to Ask About Evidence

  • Is this information accurate?
  • Where did this information come from?
  • Is the source reliable and credible?
  • Is there enough evidence to support the claim?
  • Are there other viewpoints to consider?

PEP Exam Tip

For reading comprehension questions, always go back to the text to find evidence for your answers. Don't rely on memory or outside knowledge that isn't in the passage.

Practice Questions

  1. Read a news article and identify the main idea and three supporting details.
  2. Make an inference about a character's feelings based on their actions in a story.
  3. Determine whether a text's purpose is to inform, persuade, or entertain.
  4. Evaluate the strength of evidence in an opinion article.

The Caribbean Region

The Caribbean is a region consisting of many islands in the Caribbean Sea, bounded by North America, Central America, and South America.

Physical Geography

  • Location: Between 10¬∞N to 27¬∞N latitude and 59¬∞W to 85¬∞W longitude
  • Climate: Tropical with wet and dry seasons
  • Terrain: Mostly mountainous islands with coastal plains
  • Natural hazards: Hurricanes, earthquakes, volcanic activity

  • Puerto Rico

Smaller islands in the eastern Caribbean

  • And many others

Caribbean territories on the mainland

  • Guyana

What is CARICOM?

The Caribbean Community (CARICOM) is an organization of Caribbean countries working together for economic cooperation and development.

History of CARICOM

  • 1973: CARICOM was established by the Treaty of Chaguaramas
  • Founding members: Barbados, Jamaica, Guyana, and Trinidad and Tobago
  • Growth: Now has 15 full members and 5 associate members

CARICOM Objectives

  • Promote economic integration and cooperation
  • Coordinate foreign policy among member states
  • Facilitate educational and cultural exchange
  • Work together on regional security issues
  • Protect the environment and natural resources

CARICOM Member Countries

Full Members (15 countries)

Country Capital Year Joined
Jamaica Kingston 1973
Barbados Bridgetown 1973
Trinidad and Tobago Port of Spain 1973
Guyana Georgetown 1973
Belize Belize City 1974

Benefits of CARICOM Membership

  • Free Trade: Reduced tariffs between member countries
  • Free Movement: Citizens can travel and work more easily
  • Education: Shared educational standards and qualifications
  • Disaster Response: Coordinated help during natural disasters
  • Stronger Voice: More influence in international organizations

Caribbean Culture and Identity

Shared Cultural Elements

  • Languages: English, Spanish, French, Dutch, and Creole languages
  • Music: Reggae, calypso, soca, dancehall, steelpan
  • Food: Rice and peas, jerk seasoning, roti, flying fish
  • Sports: Cricket, football (soccer), track and field
  • Festivals: Carnival, Crop Over, Independence celebrations

Historical Connections

  • Similar colonial experiences
  • Legacy of the transatlantic slave trade
  • Struggles for independence
  • Migration between Caribbean countries
Did You Know? The Caribbean has produced many world-renowned athletes, including Jamaica's Usain Bolt (fastest man in the world) and Trinidad's Keshorn Walcott (Olympic javelin champion). The region's rich cultural diversity comes from the mixing of Indigenous, African, European, and Asian influences.

Environmental Challenges

Climate Change Impacts

  • Rising sea levels threaten low-lying islands
  • More intense hurricanes and storms
  • Coral bleaching affects marine ecosystems
  • Changes in rainfall patterns affect agriculture

CARICOM's Environmental Initiatives

  • Caribbean Community Climate Change Centre
  • Regional climate adaptation strategies
  • Renewable energy projects
  • Marine protected areas
